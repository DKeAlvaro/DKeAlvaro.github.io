Human and ethical aspects of AI - Lecture 1

Written assignment (50%) & written (essay) exam (50%)

▶ Why is ethics relevant to AI design?
Because AI systems support or replace human decision-making, and ethics aims to answer how decisions should be made. (Page 5)
▶ What four features of AI require special ethical attention?
Speed, scale, opacity, and independence of human oversight. (Page 5)
▶ What forms the main source of exam questions?
The overlap between the lectures and the readings. (Page 6)
▶ What types of questions will be on the final exam?
Both open (short paragraph) and closed (MC/T-F) questions. (Page 6)
▶ What are the two ways to arrive at a correct answer on the exam?
1) Remember points directly from readings and lectures, or 2) Reason to the correct answer from your understanding of the material. (Page 6)
▶ What is the definition of ethics provided in the lecture?
Ethics is defined as the systematic study of morality. (Page 8)
▶ What are the three levels in the structure of ethics?
1) Judgments in concrete cases (morality), 2) Principles, values, virtues (ethics), 3) Theories. (Page 9)
▶ What is "Wide reflective equilibrium"?
It refers to the need to take science and empirical factors into account when seeking "reflective equilibrium" between ethical judgments, principles, and theories. (Page 9)
▶ What are the three available assignment topics?
AI-generated ethics cases [EC], AI for ethics review [AIER], and AI for Eating Disorder Symptoms [EDS]. (Page 10)
▶ What must students produce at the end of the first three weeks for the assignment?
An initial report describing the motivation, goals, requirements, and anticipated issues of the project. (Page 10)
▶ What is the primary reading for the course?
The first section of "The Cambridge Handbook of the Law, Ethics and Policy of Artificial Intelligence" (Smuha NA, ed. 2025). (Page 11)
▶ What are the two fundamental sources discussed in the lecture?
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and The Belmont Report (1979). (Page 15)
▶ When and why was the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) created?
It was created by the United Nations in 1948 in response to the moral atrocities committed in World War II. (Page 16)
▶ What does Article 12 of the UDHR protect against?
Arbitrary interference with privacy, family, home, or correspondence, and attacks upon honour and reputation. (Page 17)
▶ What is the logical relationship that defines a negative right?
$R$ has a right to $X \rightarrow S$ has a duty not to interfere with X. (Page 18)
▶ What is the logical relationship that defines a positive right?
$R$ has a right to $X \rightarrow S$ has a duty to provide X. (Page 18)
▶ What is S. Matthew Liao's "Fundamental Conditions Approach" [FCA] to human rights?
It states that "human rights protect the fundamental conditions for pursuing a good life". (Page 20)
▶ What is the full title of The Belmont Report?
Ethical Principles and Guidelines for the Protection of Human Subjects of Research. (Page 22)
▶ What is a key difference in how the Belmont Report and the UDHR are framed?
The Belmont Report is framed in terms of ethics principles rather than rights. (Page 23)
▶ What is a key similarity between the Belmont Report and the UDHR?
Both were written as a response to serious moral failures and resulted from a process of consensus building. (Page 23)
▶ What are the "Four Principles" of the approach derived from the Belmont Report?
Beneficence, Non-maleficence, Respect, and Justice. (Page 24)
▶ Why were the principles of Respect and Justice needed in addition to beneficence and non-maleficence?
Because beneficence and non-maleficence did not fully explain the wrongness of cases like Tuskegee (i.e., "using people"). (Page 27)
▶ What is an ERB?
An Ethical Review Board, which must approve all human subjects research. (Page 28)
▶ In what two ways is Artificial Intelligence considered human subjects research?
1) The data it processes is about humans, and 2) The knowledge acquired is used to drive decisions that affect human beings. (Page 29)
▶ What ethical issue was raised about facial-recognition research in the *Nature* article?
Most scientists now collect facial images (e.g., from webcams) without asking permission. (Page 30)
▶ According to the Belmont Report, what are the two ethical convictions within "Respect for persons"?
1) Individuals should be treated as autonomous agents, and 2) persons with diminished autonomy are entitled to protection. (Page 32)
▶ According to the Belmont Report, what question does the principle of "Justice" ask?
"Who ought to receive the benefits of research and bear its burdens?" (Page 32)
▶ According to EU draft guidelines, if a person makes a photograph public, does this also make the *biometric data* from that photo public?
No, it does not. (Page 33)
▶ What is the general approach the EU takes to AI policy?
A RISK-Based approach. (Page 34)
▶ The EU's Ethical AI approach is based on "Four Principles Plus One". What is the "Plus One"?
Explicability (explainability). (Page 34)
▶ What are "ethics cases" used for in ethics education?
They are descriptions of a situation used for the purpose of eliciting moral intuitions or reactions. (Page 39)
▶ What is a primary challenge with traditional ethics review that AI-supported review (Topic 2) aims to address?
Ethics review takes up considerable resources and causes delays because it is highly text-intensive. (Page 41)
▶ What is "EthicAlly"?
A research project and prototype (using Claude.ai) to explore how AI can support research ethics planning and review. (Page 45)
▶ What is the stated aim of the EthicAlly project?
To create a not-for-profit resource to provide ethics support, especially in under-resourced regions. (Page 45)
▶ What type of research can the EthicAlly beta tool *not* review?
Clinical research. (Page 46)
▶ What is the main research question for Topic 3, concerning the "fEATback" tool?
How an AI tool could be developed to support patients, family, or the public, as the current chat interface is not AI-based. (Page 50)
▶ What is "techno-solutionism" (Siffels & Sharon 2024)?
"The problem definition follows the solution and is twisted to fit the solution, rather than the other way around". (Page 51)
