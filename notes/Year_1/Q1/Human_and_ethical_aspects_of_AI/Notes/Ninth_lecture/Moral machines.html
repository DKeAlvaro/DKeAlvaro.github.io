Moral machines - Lecture 9

▶ What are the two main value paradigms for human-technology interaction discussed in the review?
Meaningful Human Control (MHC) and Human-Centered AI (HCAI) (Page 3).
▶ What ideal are MHC and HCAI contrasted with?
The Technological Autonomy Ideal (TAI) (Page 3).
▶ What is the "Technological Autonomy Ideal" (TAI) as a normative ideal?
We should strive for maximal technological autonomy in technology design (Page 5).
▶ What is the "Human Centered AI" (HCAI) ideal, according to Shneiderman (2020)?
We should strive for high automation together with high human control in technology design (Page 5).
▶ How does the lecture compare TAI and HCAI in terms of adaptation?
TAI is comparable to adapting human nature to AI, while HCAI is comparable to adapting AI and robots to human nature (Page 5).
▶ According to Shneiderman's 2x2 framework, what are the two axes?
Human Control (High/Low) and Computer Automation (High/Low) (Page 4).
▶ In Shneiderman's framework, what quadrant do "Pacemaker" and "Airbag" fall into?
Low Human Control and High Computer Automation, labeled "Computer Control" (Page 4).
▶ In Shneiderman's framework, what quadrant do "Bicycle" and "Piano" fall into?
High Human Control and Low Computer Automation, labeled "Human Mastery" (Page 4).
▶ In Shneiderman's framework, what is the ideal quadrant, labeled "Reliable, Safe & Trustworthy"?
High Human Control and High Computer Automation (Page 4).
▶ What is a key practical argument against TAI mentioned by Goldenfein et al. (2020)?
TAI will require remote operators to monitor and take back tasks, creating invisible, exploitative work (Page 6).
▶ What is a key practical problem with the HCAI ideal?
HCAI entails technology "handoffs," which raise questions about responsibility and liability (Page 6).
▶ What is a "handoff" in human-AI interaction?
A moment at which primary control transfers from one entity to another, such as from a human to automation or vice versa (Page 6, Page 22).
▶ According to Schneiderman, what three values does HCAI promote?
Reliability, Safety, and Trustworthiness (RST) (Page 7).
▶ What is the lecture's critique of Schneiderman's claim that HCAI promotes RST?
It is an empirical claim that needs a clear baseline of fully automated systems for comparison; isolated cases are not sufficient proof (Page 7).
▶ According to Schneiderman, what three characteristics of a system lead to human "Self-efficacy, mastery, and responsibility"?
Comprehensible, predictable, and controllable (Page 7).
▶ What apparent counterexample to HCAI is provided using the AlphaFold program?
It generates significant risks for scientists' ability to exercise their full capacities and utilize skills they invested years in developing (Page 8).
▶ What core value is the "Meaningful Human Control" (MHC) paradigm focused on achieving?
Responsibility (Page 10).
▶ What core value is the "Human-Centered AI" (HCAI) paradigm focused on achieving?
Trustworthiness (Page 10).
▶ What is the main conclusion of Sven Nyholm's (2020) argument?
We should adapt AI and robots to human nature, or we should adapt human nature to AI and robots (Page 12).
▶ What is an example of adapting AI to human nature in "mixed traffic"?
Make self-driving cars drive like humans (Page 18).
▶ What is an example of adapting human nature to AI in "mixed traffic"?
Seek means of making humans drive more like self-driving cars (Page 18).
▶ According to van de Poel & Royakkers, what are the 4 conditions on acceptable risk?
1. Do the benefits outweigh the costs? 2. Availability of alternatives 3. Fair distribution of risks and benefits 4. Informed consent (Page 16).
▶ What is "task displacement" in automation?
When a mechanical system takes over a task from a human, like a laundry machine (Page 22).
▶ Why is it difficult to determine responsibility during handoffs?
Because it is difficult to determine who or what is in control, and responsibility depends on an attribution of control (Page 23).
▶ What is "Level 3 automation" in assisted driving, according to Goodall (2014)?
The driver is not required to remain attentive but must be available to take control within a certain amount of time after an alert (Page 21).
▶ What is "Machine ethics" as defined by Anderson and Anderson (2007)?
Ensuring that the behavior of machines toward human users, and perhaps other machines as well, is ethically acceptable (Page 25).
▶ What is the concept of "ALIGNMENT" in recent AI discussions?
AI reasoning should be able to take into account societal values, moral and ethical considerations (Page 25, Page 26).
▶ What is Asimov's First Law of Robotics?
A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm (Page 27).
▶ What was a key conclusion from Goodall (2014) regarding AI ethics?
"There was no obvious way to encode complex human morals effectively in software" (Page 28).
▶ What are the three phases Goodall (2014) proposed for developing ethical algorithms?
1. Rational approach, 2. AI approach, 3. Natural language requirement (XAI) (Page 28).
▶ What did Goodall identify as the problem with a "Rational" (Phase 1) approach?
Rule-based ethics is too minimal, while outcome-based ethics recommends some morally bad decisions (Page 29).
▶ What did Goodall identify as the problem with an "AI-based" (Phase 2) approach?
It would reproduce human shortcomings and lack explainability (Page 29).
▶ According to Awad et al. (2020), why is it challenging to simply regulate AI?
The "Black Box" problem, technology changes faster than regulation, and the source of errors is hard to trace (Page 31).
▶ What is "SITL" as argued for by Awad et al. (2020)?
"Society in the loop," which is necessary for a dynamic consensus on the ethics of intelligent machines (Page 32).
▶ What are some of the global preferences identified by the "Moral Machines" project?
Sparing the lawful, sparing humans over pets, sparing the greater number, and sparing younger humans over older ones (Page 33).
