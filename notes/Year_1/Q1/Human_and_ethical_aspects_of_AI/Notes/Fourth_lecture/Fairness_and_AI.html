Human and ethical aspects of AI - Lecture 4

▶ What are the four approaches mentioned for incorporating ethics into AI applications?
1. Build the system using ethical rules/values; 2. Train systems on data where rules are followed; 3. Train on data where rules *might not* have been followed, but use rules as filters; 4. Rely on the socio-technical system (users) to provide ethical input (Page 5).
▶ What are some limitations of the "Design for Values" (DfV) approach?
Some ethical issues appear too late in the design process; DfV is very resource-intensive; and it may be impossible to specify values in cases of high moral uncertainty ('moral disruption') (Page 7).
▶ What is the guiding assumption of "just war theory"?
The only justification for war is self-defense (Page 9).
▶ What are the two main branches of "just war theory"?
*Jus ad bellum* (justice *of* war) and *jus in bello* (justice *in* war) (Page 9).
▶ What are two key principles of *jus in bello* (justice *in* war)?
The principles of proportionality and discrimination (Page 9).
▶ According to Cummings (2006), what is the primary purpose of analyzing ethical issues in a military system?
To identify human value design considerations that will guide the engineer in constructing a more effective decision support system (Page 10).
▶ What was the key limitation of the original Tomahawk missile?
It could not be redirected in-flight, leading to potential redundant demolition of targets or the inability to correct a targeting mistake (Page 11).
▶ What is "automation bias"?
The tendency for human operators to accept the default of automated, "smart" systems, even if they have other information that should lead them to double-check it (Page 14).
▶ What is the core argument for why algorithmic fairness must represent well-being or need?
For fairness to be non-accidental, its elements (like prior well-being, need, and group parity) must be represented in the algorithm (Page 16).
▶ What is the "Parity-based approach" to algorithmic fairness?
An approach that aims to achieve a form of parity of outcomes between different groups (Page 18).
▶ What is a "worry" or objection to the idea that fairness must be actively represented in an algorithm?
If fairness is conceptualized as "non-bias," then we only need to ensure no bias exists, not actively represent the elements of fairness (Page 21).
▶ What is "selection bias" in an algorithm?
The training data does not represent the population to which a model is applied (Page 22).
▶ What is "model bias" in an algorithm?
The model itself introduces incorrect assumptions (e.g., in what it takes as input for a risk score) (Page 22).
▶ What problem does the lecture highlight regarding the classification of "ethnicity"?
The technical assumptions of a classification system demand mutually exclusive categories, which is problematic for a concept like race and may not capture reality (Page 23).
▶ What are the problems with using "fairness as algorithmic non-bias" (i.e., a "blind" model)?
Background inequalities are left in place, and "proxies" for a characteristic (like race) might be included accidentally or intentionally (Page 24).
▶ What is the difference between "procedural fairness" and "substantive fairness"?
Procedural concepts describe *how* decisions should be made (the process), while substantive notions refer to the *outcome* or goal the decision should achieve (Page 25).
▶ What is the difference between "Contractualism" and "Contractarianism"?
Contractualism (Hobbes) is what people *actually* agree to, while Contractarianism (Rawls) is what *reasonable* people *would* agree to under ideal circumstances (Page 26).
▶ What is the "Veil of Ignorance" in Rawlsian justice?
A hypothetical condition where people agree on principles of justice *without* knowing which position in society they will go on to occupy (Page 27).
▶ What is the "Difference Principle"?
Inequalities are permitted only when they benefit those who are least well off (Page 28).
▶ What is "Prioritarianism"?
Increases in well-being for those who are less well-off count more than similar increases for those who are better off (Page 28).
▶ What is the "Rawlsian rule for algorithmic fairness" proposed by Barsotti & Kocer (2024)?
'A model is fair if it does not make more systematic errors for any [relevant] sub-group in the dataset compared to the others' (Page 29).
▶ What is the main argument from Winters et al. (2020) regarding digital health and justice?
Based on Prioritarianism, we should favor contextual and targeted initiatives because they benefit the poor more, even if they require more resources than general, scalable tools (Page 33).
▶ What is "relational justice"?
An approach to justice that focuses on power and history instead of a general principle of distribution (Page 34).
▶ What slogan is associated with relational justice in data ethics?
"Nothing about us, without us" (Page 34).
▶ What was the Havasupai tribe case about?
Their DNA samples, collected for diabetes studies, had been used without consent for other genetic studies (Page 35).
▶ What is "punitive justice"?
Justice related to decisions about jail or sentencing, guided by the principle that "justice is blind" to arbitrary characteristics (Page 36).
▶ What is the argument against using statistical tools like the Public Safety Assessment (PSA) for detention?
We should not deny people their liberty on statistical grounds; individualized evidence should be required (Page 37).
▶ What advice does the lecture give to AI experts who want to be useful to the world?
To "dive into" the domain (e.g., study the history of segregation if you care about housing) and not be "domain agnostic" (Page 38).
