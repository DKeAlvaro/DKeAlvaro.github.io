Two value paradigms for human-AI interaction - Lecture 8

▶ What is a "responsibility gap"?
A concern that nobody can be reasonably held responsible for bad outcomes caused by a (semi-)autonomous system (Page 3).
▶ What are the three conditions an entity must satisfy to be held responsible?
Foreknowledge ("knew or should have known"), Control/causal influence, and Freedom ("could have done otherwise") (Page 3).
▶ What is the general definition of "Meaningful Human Control" (MHC) according to Santoni di Sio & van den Hoven (2018)?
"humans not computers and their algorithms should ultimately remain in control of, and thus morally responsible for, relevant decisions" (Page 4).
▶ According to Fischer & Ravizza, what are the two dimensions of control?
Guidance control and Regulative control (Page 7).
▶ What is "guidance control"?
It is based on reason-responsiveness and the attributability of an outcome to an agent (Page 7).
▶ What is "regulative control"?
It is based on the idea that the agent could have acted differently (freedom) (Page 7).
▶ Which dimension of control from Fischer & Ravizza is the basis for MHC?
Guidance control (Page 8).
▶ Which dimension of control from Fischer & Ravizza is *not* required for MHC?
Regulative control (Page 8, Page 9).
▶ What are the two aspects of Meaningful Human Control?
Tracking and Tracing (Page 8, Page 9).
▶ What is the "tracking" condition in MHC?
It is the "reason-responsiveness" aspect of guidance control (Page 8).
▶ What is the "tracing" condition in MHC?
It is the "each outcome is attributable to an agent" aspect of guidance control (Page 8).
▶ What are the four conditions for Nozick's "tracking" account of knowledge?
(i) P is true. (ii) S believes P. (iii) If P were not true, S would not believe P. (iv) If P were true, S would believe P (Page 10).
▶ What is the "tracing" condition based on from Fischer & Ravizza?
"Ownership" of an action or decision (Page 12).
▶ Why can a machine apparently not be responsible?
It does not satisfy the freedom condition, and there is a "retribution gap" (it doesn't make sense to blame or punish a machine) (Page 16).
▶ According to Santoni di Sio & van den Hoven, can humans be held responsible for actions delegated to smart machines?
Yes, "Delegation of activity to smart machines is compatible with genuine responsibility attributions" (Page 17).
▶ What is the main idea of MHC regarding responsibility?
When guidance control is satisfied by automation, the humans who built and operate the system can be held responsible (Page 17).
▶ What is the implication of the "tracing" condition for designers?
Designers must ensure human agents in the chain are "technically and psychologically capable" and "well aware of their responsibility" (Page 24).
▶ In the Tesla example where the driver doesn't swerve, is she responsible even if the autopilot would have blocked her?
MHC predicts yes, because she has guidance control (Page 25).
▶ In the reverse Tesla example where the autopilot doesn't swerve, are the designers responsible even if the human would have blocked it?
MHC predicts yes, because the autopilot has guidance control and the outcome is a foreseeable consequence of its design (Page 26).
▶ How does the lecture map the traditional conditions for responsibility to the MHC framework?
Foreknowledge -> guidance control, tracking; Control/causal influence -> guidance control, tracing; Freedom -> regulative control (Page 27).
▶ What is the "one-dimensional model" of automation?
A linear scale from "Complete human control" to "Autonomous mechanical control" (Page 29).
▶ What does the one-dimensional model of automation suggest?
A tradeoff between human control and automation (Page 30).
▶ What is the "two-dimensional model" of automation, according to Shneiderman (2020)?
A model with two axes: "More automation" (horizontal) and "More human control" (vertical) (Page 31).
▶ In Shneiderman's 2D framework, what is the ideal quadrant?
"Reliable, Safe & Trustworthy," characterized by High Human Control and High Computer Automation (Page 32).
▶ What is the "Technological Autonomy Ideal" (TAI)?
The normative ideal that we should strive for maximal technological autonomy in technology design (Page 33).
▶ What is the "Human Centered AI" (HCAI) ideal?
The normative ideal that we should strive for high automation together with high human control in technology design (Page 33).
▶ How do TAI and HCAI relate to human adaptation?
TAI implies adapting human nature to AI, while HCAI implies adapting AI and robots to human nature (Page 33).
▶ What is one of Shneiderman's critiques of the TAI?
"Humans have to spend more effort monitoring autonomous computers because they are unsure what it will do, often leading to inferior performance." (Page 34).
▶ What is another critique of TAI listed by Shneiderman?
"Computers are not teammates, collaborators, or co-active partners... Humans are responsible for actions of the technology assists that they use..." (Page 34).
▶ What is a key practical problem with TAI according to Goldenfein et al. (2020)?
It will require remote operators for monitoring, creating invisible and exploitative work (Page 35).
▶ What is a key practical problem with HCAI according to Goldenfein et al. (2020)?
It entails technology "handoffs" which create questions about responsibility and liability (Page 35).
▶ What is a "handoff" (or "handback")?
A moment at which primary control transfers from one entity to another (e.g., from human to automation or vice versa) (Page 36).
▶ What values does HCAI promote, according to Shneiderman?
Reliability, safety, and trustworthiness (Page 37).
▶ According to Shneiderman, what human values are supported by systems that are comprehensible, predictable, and controllable?
Self-efficacy, mastery, and responsibility (Page 37).
▶ What is the "apparent counterexample to HCAI" involving AlphaFold?
While it helps scientists, it also generates risks for their ability to exercise their full capacities and demonstrate mastery of skills they spent years developing (Page 38).
▶ What are the shared claims of MHC, HCAI, and related paradigms?
High automation can be combined with human contributions to achieve values like Responsibility (MHC) and Trustworthiness (HCAI) (Page 40).
▶ What is the relationship between trust and "discretionary authority" in professional ethics?
Trust is essential because it is the "willingness to make oneself vulnerable to the discretionary choices of another person" (Page 43).
▶ What is Baier's (2013) argument about transparency and trust?
Increasing monitoring and supervision (transparency) is an act of *decreasing* trust, not building it (Page 46).
